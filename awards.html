The Christopher N.H. Jenkins Cancer Control Award is an annual, endowed award to recognize an individual who has made significant accomplishments in community-oriented cancer prevention and control efforts targeting Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders. The Award will be presented annually at a meeting in the Fall in San Francisco, CA.

Christopher N. H. Jenkins

A native of Pennsylvania, graduated from Stanford University in 1966. He spent 2 years in Vietnam with International Voluntary Services, doing community development work. He then pursued a Masters Degree in Asian Studies from the University of California, Berkeley. In 1971, he married his wife, Tran Khanh-Tuyet, whom he had met in Vietnam. In 1982, he returned to the UC Berkeley and received his Masters Degree in Public Health in 1984. From 1984-2001, Mr. Jenkins worked at UC San Francisco. In 1986, he and his colleague, Dr. Stephen McPhee, started the Vietnamese Community Health Promotion Project (VCHPP). With funding from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the National Cancer Institute, and the State of California, among others, the Project has developed and evaluated interventions to promote smoking cessation and prevention, proper nutrition, screening for breast, cervical, and colon cancer, and hepatitis B screening and immunizations among Vietnamese. In 1995, Mr. Jenkins conducted the first national smoking prevalence survey in Vietnam. In 1998, he returned to Vietnam to advise the Ministry of Health in developing a national tobacco control policy. He and the VCHPP staff have published their research findings in the Journal of the American Medical Association, American Journal of Public Health, Preventive Medicine, and American Journal of Preventive Medicine, among others. Prior to his untimely death in 2001, Mr. Jenkins served as an advisor to the Asian Pacific Partners for Empowerment and Leadership and the Intercultural Cancer Council, and on the Board of Directors of the Asian Pacific Islander American Health Forum.

Thoa Nguyen

Thoa Nguyen is a pioneer in Asian American health and community-based participatory research (CBPR). From 1992 to 2010, Thoa worked for the Vietnamese Community Health Promotion Project (VCHPP) or Sức Khoẻ Là Vàng!, a community-academic advocacy and research unit at UC San Francisco. She was project director of research projects on cancer screening, tobacco control, hepatitis B, nutrition, and cardiovascular risk factors in the Bay Area, California, DC, and Texas. With her phenomenal energy, remarkable organizational skills, and extensive social networks, Thoa helped VCHPP obtain and carry out 65 grants worth over $36 million. In 1999, Thoa brought together a dozen organizations to create the Vietnamese Reach for Health Coalition in Santa Clara County, CA. VCHPP and the Coalition conducted CDC-funded CBPR to promote cervical and breast cancer screening among Vietnamese Americans. Thoa’s organizational, cultural, and people skills brought a diverse group of people together and helped it to thrive for 20 years despite competing priorities and challenging environments. For 30 years as a provider, community organizer, and researcher, Thoa Nguyen promoted health research and equity for Asian Americans. As Rod Lew stated, "Thoa modeled how to interact with community not as 'research subjects' but as respectable people, partners, community leaders with a voice that needed to be heard... she was always a strong advocate for understanding the cultural beliefs and practices of the Vietnamese people.

Thu Quach

Thu Quach is a Research Scientist at the Cancer Prevention Institute of California, where she leads several studies focusing on the booming nail salon workforce, many of whom are Vietnamese immigrant women. She also works on community-based participatory research projects (CBPR) to characterize environmental hazards in immigrant communities. In addition, she is also Director of Community Health and Research at Asian Health Services, a community health center in Oakland Chinatown, where she works with providers and other community health staff to promote research that would inform practice-based and community-based efforts to address health disparities in the Asian and Pacific Islander population.

Stephen McPhee, MD

Dr. McPhee is a pioneer in the fields of Asian American health research and community-based participatory research. Together with Christopher Jenkins, he established one of the first community-academic research units on Asian American health, the Vietnamese Community Health Promotion Project (Suc Khoe La Vang!) at UCSF in 1986. Dr. McPhee conducted the first community-wide intervention trials and randomized controlled trials of health promotion interventions such as mass media and lay health workers outreach among Vietnamese Americans on topics such as tobacco control, breast and cervical cancer screening, colorectal cancer screening, as well as hepatitis B vaccination and screening. He was the San Francisco Regional Principal Investigator of the Asian American Cancer Research Awareness, Research, and Training (AANCART) from 2000 to 2008. In his career, Dr. McPhee obtained nearly 50 grants and over $36 million in research funding and published more than 80 research articles on Asian American health. In addition to his work with Asian American health, Dr. McPhee is also a pioneer in the field of palliative care, having cofounded the UCSF Palliative Care Service in 1999 and editing a series called "Perspectives on Care at the Close of Life” in the Journal of the American Medical Association from 2000 to 2008. A UCSF Department of Medicine Master Clinician, Dr. McPhee has been the lead editor of many core medicine textbooks.

Elisa K. Tong, MA, MD

Dr. Tong is a leader in cancer prevention and control among Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders, particularly in the area of tobacco control. She served as the Research Director for the Sacramento site of the Asian American Cancer Research Awareness, Research, and Training (AANCART) and launched the Sacramento Asian Pacific Islander Cancer Caucus. Dr. Tong has exposed the tobacco industry’s efforts that undermined linking secondhand smoke with cancer and other diseases, in efforts to prevent global smoke-free policies. She has described smoking behavior among Asian Americans in California and showed that smoke-free policies are particularly effective for Asian American smokers' cessation and nonsmokers' protection. She has worked in partnership with San Francisco Chinatown Public Health Center and Asian Pacific Partners for Empowerment Advocacy and Leadership on AANHPI community-based tobacco research projects. She has also worked on international tobacco control projects in China. Dr. Tong initiated UC Quits, the UC-wide Tobacco Cessation Network, and launched CA Quits, a statewide tobacco cessation learning collaborative. She also led statewide outreach to increase calls by Medi- Cal members who smoke to the California Smokers' Helpline, which offers Asian language lines. She has received the UC Davis School of Medicine Dean’s Team Excellence Award, the American Legacy Foundation Sybil G. Jacobs Award for Outstanding Use of Tobacco Industry Documents, and the American Public Health Association Asian Pacific Islander Caucus' "Best Published Paper" Award.
